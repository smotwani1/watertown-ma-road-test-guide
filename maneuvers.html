Maneuvers & scoring

It's a points system, one small slip rarely fails you. But the items below are where most people quietly lose, so learn what the examiner is actually watching.

What the examiner is grading

The five that decide most results:

  • ✓
    Visual checks, the big one. The official skill is "look carefully before changing lanes." In practice, examiners want to see you shoulder- and mirror-check before every lane change, turn, and reverse. Make your looking obvious.
  • ✓
    Complete stops. Stop fully at the line. Test-takers advise a deliberate ~3-second stop so it's clearly complete.
  • ✓
    Signal before every change. Every turn and lane change, even on an empty street.
  • ✓
    Posture & lane. Both hands on the wheel; turn into the lane you turned from.
  • ✓
    Yield correctly. Right-of-way, safe following distance, safe speeds for conditions.

The maneuvers

How to do each one and the most common way people fail it.

Parallel parking

The maneuver most people fear, and the one Watertown always tests. The official RMV method, step by step: Official

  1. Pick a space long enough for your car, where parking is legal.
  2. Pull up alongside the car in front of the space, ~2–3 feet away, with your rear bumper or front seats lined up with theirs.
  3. Look behind you, both ways, for pedestrians and traffic.
  4. Back up slowly, turning the wheel all the way toward the curb. Foot light on the brake; look out your rear window, not the mirrors.
  5. Once your front clears the parked car's rear bumper, turn the wheel the opposite way and keep backing in.
  6. When you're far enough in, straighten the wheels and pull forward, leaving room front and back.
  7. Shift into park.

Most common fail: not checking the right shoulder before backing in (step 3), and ending up too far from the curb.

One real tip: timing shifts with car size, a method dialed in for a sedan may not transfer to an SUV. Practice in the exact car you'll test in.

Three-point turn

Done on Berkley St, near the CrossFit gym. If a car comes toward you mid-turn, don't panic, one recent test-taker simply finished the maneuver and let the other driver back up. Composure is part of the grade. Reports

Most common fail: forgetting to shift into reverse for the middle step under nerves.

Back up straight (~50 ft)

Look out the rear window and use your mirrors to stay straight, the steering wheel won't tell you anything useful. Drifting onto the curb or grass counts as hitting the curb. Official

Hill parking

Show the correct wheel direction parked uphill and downhill, with and without a curb. Remember UCLA, Uphill, Curb, Left, Always; everything else turns toward the curb. Official
